# Calibration of Firms Parameters

## Aggregate Production Function and Capital Accumulation

The [OG-Core firm theory documentation](https://pslmodels.github.io/OG-Core/content/theory/firms.html) outlines the constant returns to scale, constant elasticity of substitution production function of the representative firm.  This function has two parameters; the elasticity of substitution and capital's share of output.

The production function is given as:

```{math}
:label: EqFirmsCESprodfun
  \begin{split}
    Y_{m,t} &= F(K_{m,t}, K_{g,m,t}, L_{m,t}) \\
    &\equiv Z_{m,t}\biggl[(\gamma_m)^\frac{1}{\varepsilon_m}(K_{m,t})^\frac{\varepsilon_m-1}{\varepsilon_m} + (\gamma_{g,m})^\frac{1}{\varepsilon_m}(K_{g,m,t})^\frac{\varepsilon_m-1}{\varepsilon_m} + \\
    &\quad\quad\quad\quad\quad(1-\gamma_m-\gamma_{g,m})^\frac{1}{\varepsilon_m}(e^{g_y t}L_{m,t})^\frac{\varepsilon_m-1}{\varepsilon_m}\biggr]^\frac{\varepsilon_m}{\varepsilon_m-1} \quad\forall m,t
  \end{split}
```

  This production function has the following parameters:
  * $\varepsilon_m$ is the elasticity of substitution between capital, labor, and infrastructure in sector $m$.
  * $\gamma_m$ is the share of capital in sector $m$.
  * $\gamma_{g,m}$ is the share of government capital in sector $m$.
  * $Z_{m,t}$ is the total factor productivity in sector $m$ at time $t$.

### Elasticity of substitution

`OG-ZAF`'s default parameterization has an elasticity of substitution of $\varepsilon=1.0$, which implies a Cobb-Douglas production function, in both the single-industry and multi-industry calibrations.

### Factor shares of output

We set infrastructure's share of output to $\gamma_{g,m}=0.0$ for all sectors.  This parameter is hard to identify from national accounts data and would entail an empirical study to tease out the relationship between infrastructure and output.

In the single-industry calibration, capital's share of output is $\gamma = 0.47164$, one minus labour's share of value added (a labour share of $0.528$ from the [UN ILOSTAT database](https://rshiny.ilo.org/dataexplorer9/?lang=en)).

## Multi-industry calibration

`OG-ZAF` ships a multi-industry calibration alongside the single-industry default, in its own file `ogzaf/ogzaf_default_parameters_multisector.json`, leaving the single-industry `ogzaf_default_parameters.json` untouched. The multi-industry file is *self-sufficient*: it is the single-industry calibration with the multi-industry parameters merged in, so it loads standalone in one step, exactly like the single-industry default (`examples/run_og_zaf_multiple_industry.py`). It is (re)generated by `ogzaf/create_multisector_calibration.py` from the underlying data. Because the economy-wide South African values are copied into it, regenerate this file whenever the single-industry base is recalibrated, so the two representations stay in step.

The data source is the 2019 South African Social Accounting Matrix (SASAM) published by UNU-WIDER {cite}`SASAM2023`, which distinguishes 61 production activities and 108 commodities. These are aggregated to $M=8$ production industries and $I=5$ consumption goods using the concordances in `ogzaf/constants.py` (`PROD_DICT`, `CONS_DICT`). The eight industries are Agriculture, Mining, Electricity, Water and Waste, Construction, Trade/Transport/Accommodation, Services, and Manufacturing. Electricity is kept as its own industry so energy policy can be studied directly; Manufacturing is placed **last** because OG-Core makes the last industry the price numeraire and the sole producer of all non-consumption final demand (investment and government), so its nominal output share is mechanically inflated and is not comparable to its value-added share.

### Capital shares $\gamma_m$

The SASAM reports one capital row (gross operating surplus) and four labour rows. Each industry's raw capital share is operating surplus over total factor income. These raw shares carry the cross-industry pattern the SAM identifies, but their level is biased upward (self-employed mixed income is booked to capital), so we rescale them by a common factor to set the value-added-weighted mean to the single-industry $\gamma = 0.47164$. This keeps the two representations sharing one capital-share level while preserving the industry dispersion.

| Industry | $\gamma_m$ | | Industry | $\gamma_m$ |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Agriculture | 0.592 | | Trade/Transport/Accom. | 0.516 |
| Mining | 0.577 | | Services | 0.427 |
| Electricity | 0.664 | | Manufacturing | 0.525 |
| Water and Waste | 0.433 | | Construction | 0.308 |

Construction is the most labour-intensive (lowest $\gamma_m$); Electricity, capital-intensive power generation, is the most capital-intensive.

### Total factor productivity $Z_m$

Industry TFP is the Solow residual of the production function,

```{math}
:label: EqFirmsZsolow
  Z_m = \frac{Y_m}{K_m^{\gamma_m}\,L_m^{1-\gamma_m-\gamma_{g,m}}},
```

with $Y_m$ industry value added (SASAM), $L_m$ a physical head count from the Statistics South Africa Quarterly Labour Force Survey (2019 annual average) measured independently of the SAM's factor payments, and $K_m$ the national capital stock (a capital-output ratio of $2.9$, Penn World Table, times total value added) allocated across industries by their share of capital income. The QLFS "Electricity, gas and water" employment aggregate is split between Electricity and Water and Waste by the SASAM's labour-compensation shares, since waste and sanitation are labour-intensive while power generation is not.

The relative TFPs are normalized so the numeraire industry (Manufacturing) equals one. Because the numeraire=1 convention fixes the relative TFPs but leaves the income *level* free, and that convention lands the eight-industry economy's aggregate productivity above the single-industry model's, the whole $Z_m$ vector is then multiplied by a common Hicks-neutral constant (0.865). This leaves relative prices, sector shares and $\gamma_m$ dispersion unchanged and moves only the income level, calibrated so the model's solved `factor` (mean income in the data over mean income in the model) matches the single-industry model's — the two representations must share `factor` because it scales the incomes at which the progressive income tax is evaluated.

| Industry | $Z_m$ | | Industry | $Z_m$ |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Agriculture | 0.316 | | Trade/Transport/Accom. | 0.731 |
| Mining | 0.776 | | Services | 1.876 |
| Electricity | 0.444 | | Manufacturing | 0.865 |
| Water and Waste | 3.047 | | Construction | 1.828 |

### Consumption goods: `alpha_c` and the `io_matrix`

Households allocate spending across the $I=5$ consumption goods (Food; Energy and extraction; Non-durables; Durables; Services) with Cobb-Douglas budget shares `alpha_c`, calibrated from household final consumption in the SASAM:

| Consumption good | `alpha_c` |
|---|---|
| Food | 0.202 |
| Energy and extraction | 0.028 |
| Non-durables | 0.164 |
| Durables | 0.080 |
| Services | 0.526 |

The `io_matrix` ($I\times M$, rows summing to one) maps each consumption good to the industries that produce its value added. OG-Core's production side has no intermediate inputs, so the object needed is the *domestic value added embodied in final consumption*. We build it with standard industry-technology make/use algebra derived from the SASAM: the market-share matrix allocates each commodity's domestic supply to producing activities, the domestic Leontief inverse traces the supply chain, and value added per unit of gross output converts activity output into value added. Household consumption of each commodity, scaled to its domestic-supply share (imports carry no domestic value added), weights the commodities within each good. The result is intuitive: a rand of Food is mostly Manufacturing (food processing) and Services (distribution) with an Agriculture core; Energy and extraction loads heavily on Electricity; Services is overwhelmingly the Services industry.

### Preferences under multiple goods

OG-Core's composite-consumption price index is unnormalized, so moving from one consumption good to five shrinks composite-consumption units by a constant $k=\prod_i \alpha_{c,i}^{-\alpha_{c,i}}$. Because the utility weights $\chi^n_s$ and $\chi^b_j$ are fixed numbers set in single-industry units, the overlay scales both by $k^{\sigma-1}$ — a derived units conversion, not a re-tune — so multi-industry households remain behaviourally identical to the single-industry baseline.

### Consistency with the single-industry calibration

Solving both steady states, the multi-industry economy reproduces the single-industry targets and level. Debt and its foreign share, and the tax rates, match by construction; `factor` (the level diagnostic), the capital-output ratio, and the nominal consumption share come out close; the interest rate sits modestly higher, as is typical of the multi-industry representation.
